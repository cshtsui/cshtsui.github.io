Interruption Design

Interruption components provide users timely contextual awareness of important information related to their job. These components can communicate conditions, indicate an event, or show responses to user actions.

This page documents general design guidelines for interruption components used in athenanet products. These components draw attention to information from the applications (either as a result of user input or generated by the system itself) that needs users' consideration.

In the Forge design system, interruption components and patterns include Tooltip, StatusTag, Banner, Toast, Modal, and form error validation.

General Design Guidelines

Design using Forge interruption components should be:

  • Appropriately interruptive
    Notifications and indicators can be interruptive, but their level of interruption should align with the level of urgency. Ask how important it is for users to stop their current workflow and take action. Carefully choose the interruption level to reduce alert fatigue.

  • Informative
    Interruption components should be direct in communicating what happened. If there's a solution, explain it and provide a means to execute it (e.g., CTA button). If there's no immediate solution, give troubleshooting instructions. Use Forge alert colors and/or icons in the design for quick recognition.

  • Efficient
    The content of the interruption message should be precise and brief. For example, use numbers, dates, and short phrases for quick reading and comprehension.

  • Noticeable
    When possible, interruption components should be visually near the source of the issue. Except for Modal, placement of interruption components should not hinder users' interaction with other elements on the screen. For components that dismiss themselves, such as Toast, consider providing a way for users to retrieve their messages after they are gone from screen (e.g., a sliding notification panel).

  • Multidimensional
    Interruption design should consider using a combination of color, placement, timing, frequency, and component choice (Modal, Toast, Banner, etc.) to convey information effectively. Make sure it is quickly recognized and understood by the users.

  • Consistent
    Apply visual and interaction patterns consistently across the products to avoid user confusion. For example, if one area calls attention to abnormal vitals by displaying them in red text with an alert icon, it would be inconsistent to call attention to them in another area by displaying them in the Banner component.

Design Process

1. Determine the level of interruption

The first step of interruption design is to determine the level of interruption: the level of attention that users should give to the information being highlighted. When deciding, use the Interruption Scale (below) and consider these factors:

  • Severity of consequences: Non-critical vs. critical
  • Timeliness of reaction: No action required vs. immediate action required
  • Duration of the message: Temporary vs. persistent
  • Frequency of the message: High (e.g., StatusTag), medium, or low (e.g., Modal)

Determine where your use case fits on the scale and select the type of interruption that best fits your situation.

Interruption Type Example
Nested

2. Choose the right component

The next step is to decide which Forge component is appropriate for the type of interruption you need. Consider the following:

  • Format and interaction behavior
    For example, Modal will stop users from interacting with other elements on the screen, Toast will be displayed briefly while users continue to work on other areas of the screen, and StatusTag will be persistently displayed and not affect user workflow.

  • Amount of information to display
    For example, a label with or without an icon, a short sentence, a message with action buttons, etc.

  • Context of the message
    Should the message be displayed at the page level, at the section level, or inline?

  • Potential for alert fatigue
    Be very cautious when choosing a high-urgency interruption component such as Modal. It will severely disrupt users' workflow and add to their mental stress. Excessive amounts of warning messages on a screen or Modals surfacing in high frequency will desensitize users (due to sensory overload) and lead to alert fatigue.

3. Define the details

After an interruption component is chosen, the final step is to define details that will dial the strength of the interruption up or down. These include color and tone of voice.

Forge Alert Color Semantics

 
 Success
The action that the user took has been executed successfully.

Examples: Action completed, content saved
 
 Information
System- or user-generated, something that the user should know that they may or may not need to take action on; additional information provided by athena about something's function or meaning

Examples: Arrival of lab results, an explanation for a Medicare abbreviation
 
 Attention
Something that needs action that the user should be aware of; an error or something that has the potential to become an error situation; mild or moderate clinical/patient safety risk

Examples: Missed required fields, mild medication warning
 
 Critical
Reserved to indicate the highest risk and most severe issues

Examples: Abnormal results that may cause patient harm, moderate or severe medication warnings, a financial issue that would cause a client to lose Medicare payments

Accessibility

When using color, remember that color by itself is not enough to show alert level. Color alone is not accessible; users who can't see or perceive color won't be able to tell how important the message is. Use colors and icons to express alert level.

Forge components have this accessibility built in. For example, Toast and Banner use both colors and icons to show alert level.

Multiple Interruption Messages

In addition to focusing on the details of individual interruption components, designers should consider them in the context of the entire screen and workflow.

When multiple notifications need to be surfaced, consider their visual hierarchy on the screen so that users can quickly identify and direct their attention to the most urgent ones.

When appropriate, group interruption messages of the same type to make it easier for users to follow. For example, instead of multiple separated banners, combine multiple attention-level warning messages in a single page-level banner.

Product-specific Interruption Needs

Forge's interruption design guidelines are generic and aim to cover the basics for the wide array of athenaNet products, including Clinicals, Collector, Consumer Health, and more.

When developing interruption messages for ambulatory and hospital applications, please refer to established design patterns as well, such as the Drug-Drug Interaction (DDI) Alert scheme and the Emergency Severity Index guidelines (Hospital and ER settings). Please consult athenahealth's Patient Safety Team for additional instructions and guidelines.
